Do you know what "NANO" means?

Nanoscience and Nanotechnology refer to the synthesis, fabrication, characterization, manipulation, modeling, and study of nanomaterials (NMs). However, the definition of nanomaterials is not completely standardized around the world. A nanomaterial is a substance (a natural, incidental, or manufactured material) whose size is between 1-100 nanometers (nm), in at least one dimension.
For example, the average size of a normal soccer ball is 22 centimeters (cm), the size of some ants is 52 millimeters (mm) and the average size of a virus is 10 nanometers (nm).
On this page, we will share information related to the nano universe (nanoverse), that means in the nanoscale!

Why NANO? Which is its importance?

Engineered or manufactured nanomaterials may present different physicochemical properties from the bulk materials. For example, GOLD changes its color when we work in nanoscale; gold nanoparticles are of distinct colors depending on the size of the particles.
These characteristics have the potential to increase the development of different areas, for example in the pharmaceutical industry, electronics, medicine, environmental remediation, etc.

Nanomaterials in the market 💸

Nowadays, we can find different nanomaterials in different sectors of industry, already marketed, and available in our hands. For example, in the automotive industry, in communications, in chemistry, in pharmaceutics, in the energy sector, or in environmental remediation.
For simplistic purposes, we will refer to those as "nano-products" 🙌

Some nanometerials could cross the blood-brain barrier (BBB)

So far, there is evidence that shows some nanomaterials can reach the bloodstream and, then, can cross the BBB, after being inhaled or consumed orally. However, as mentioned in a previous post, it depends on different factors.

Reference:

  • Jiménez-Barrios, N., & Chirino, Y. I. (2018). ¿Los nanomateriales pueden causar neurotoxicidad?. Mundo nano. Revista interdisciplinaria en nanociencias y nanotecnología, 11(20), 17-26.

Which factors affect the toxicity of nanomaterials?

There are several factors that affect the potential toxicity of a nanomaterial. Such as, size, shape, surface chemistry, surface charge, dissolution, the kind of material, among others. As we can see, there are many. That's why it could be considered an emerging contaminant.

References:

  • Álvarez-Muñoz, D., Llorca, M., Blasco, J., & Barceló, D. (2016). Contaminants in the marine environment. In Marine ecotoxicology (pp. 1-34). Academic Press.
  • Pawar, S., Sardesai, M., & Shende, P. (2021). Nanotoxicological Approaches Towards Nanosafety. Nanotoxicology and Nanoecotoxicology Vol. 2, 195-224.

Nanomaterials, emerging contaminants? Environmental risks? 😮

Some authors comment that the presence of nanomaterials, or sub-products, in the environment has been reported. This is the result of their (direct or indirect) release from manufacturing processes, consumer goods, or applications. Hence, nanomaterials could be mentioned as emerging pollutants 🌍
However, how do we know if a nanomaterial is of concern? 🤔 As mentioned in a previous post, NANOTOXICOLOGY could help us to determine the potential "damage". In addition, NANOECOTOXICOLOGY studies, evaluates, and predicts the possible impact on biological organisms and/or their ecological effects 🥳

What about the legislation in Mexico?

In Mexico, The Technical Committee for National Standardization in Nanotechnologies (CTNNN, by its acronym in Spanish) has the function of preparing the voluntary Mexican standards (NMX) for nanotechnologies. However, the obligatory rules (NOM) have not been stablished yet. In addition, the country does not have any legislation related to chemical compounds (as you can see in the video), in which nanomaterials might be included.
